2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The modern retail and e-commerce environment is characterized by hyper-competition, rising customer acquisition costs, and increasingly thin profit margins. In order to survive and scale, retail organizations can no longer rely on intuition-based pricing or blanket promotional strategies. Instead, they must leverage data to understand exactly which levers drive top-line revenue and bottom-line profitability. This capstone project was initiated to address a critical ambiguity within the business: leadership needed concrete, statistically validated evidence regarding how their ongoing discounting strategies and omnichannel investments (Online vs. In-Store) were actually impacting total revenue and the Average Order Value (AOV). 

To solve this, our team engineered a robust, end-to-end data analytics pipeline leveraging Python (pandas, numpy, scipy, statsmodels) and Tableau. We utilized a comprehensive Kaggle Retail Store Sales dataset encompassing approximately 12,500 transactions spanning the years 2022 to 2025. The analytical lifecycle involved rigorous Data Extraction, Transformation, and Loading (ETL), followed by deep Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA). Because visual trends can often be misleading, we subjected our visual findings to a battery of advanced statistical tests, including the Shapiro-Wilk test for normality, the Mann-Whitney U test for non-parametric variance, One-Way ANOVA for categorical revenue drivers, Chi-Squared tests for categorical independence, and Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Multiple Linear Regression to model the precise drivers of customer spend.

The insights generated from this rigorous pipeline are striking. We discovered that the business is heavily reliant on promotions, with approximately 33% of all transactions utilizing a discount. However, statistical testing unequivocally proved that while these discounts do yield a mathematically significant lift in the Average Order Value, the actual monetary value of that lift is practically negligible (less than $2 per transaction). In essence, the business is subsidizing purchases that would have likely occurred anyway, unnecessarily eroding profit margins. Furthermore, we identified that transaction quantity and product category are the true, dominant drivers of high-value carts, and that promotional strategies are currently misaligned between the Online and In-Store channels.

Based on these irrefutable findings, we present three core strategic directives to executive leadership: 
First, the business must immediately transition away from flat-rate percentage discounts and implement threshold-based discounting (e.g., "Spend $150 to unlock a 10% discount"). This forces an increase in transaction quantity—the proven driver of revenue—to unlock the deal, thereby protecting baseline margins. 
Second, marketing and inventory budgets must be reallocated to heavily favor the top-performing product categories identified in our ANOVA testing, while bottom-tier inventory should be slated for liquidation. 
Third, the business must unify its omnichannel presence by standardizing promotional offers across both digital and physical storefronts, ensuring a cohesive customer experience and balanced channel growth.

3. SECTOR CONTEXT AND PROBLEM STATEMENT

Sector Context
The global retail and e-commerce sector is currently navigating a period of significant macroeconomic turbulence. Over the past few years, inflationary pressures have altered consumer spending habits, making shoppers more price-sensitive and deal-seeking. Consequently, retailers have increasingly relied on discounts, flash sales, and aggressive promotional campaigns to maintain transaction volumes and clear warehousing inventory. However, this reliance on discounting represents a "race to the bottom." While promotions can artificially inflate short-term sales volume, they severely degrade profit margins, brand equity, and customer lifetime value if not executed with surgical precision. 

Concurrently, the retail landscape has permanently shifted toward an omnichannel model. The dichotomy between "Online" and "In-Store" shopping has dissolved into a fluid customer journey where consumers expect seamless integration between digital browsing and physical purchasing. Retailers must carefully balance their capital expenditure and operational budgets between these two channels. Over-investing in physical infrastructure while digital sales surge, or over-subsidizing online deliveries while physical stores lay dormant, can lead to severe financial distress. Therefore, a data-driven understanding of how promotions interact with these channels is not just an academic exercise; it is a fundamental requirement for corporate survival.

Problem Statement
Despite having access to historical transaction data, the executive leadership team currently lacks a clear, quantitative understanding of how their promotional strategies and channel infrastructures are performing. There is a persistent assumption within the marketing department that frequent discounting is the primary mechanism for driving large cart sizes (Average Order Value). Furthermore, there is ambiguity regarding whether the Online or In-Store channel yields a higher return on investment, and whether product categories behave differently depending on the sales channel. 

The core objective of this project is to rigorously quantify the impact of discounting and channel choice on total revenue and AOV. We must definitively answer whether the current promotional strategy is accretive or dilutive to margins, and we must identify the specific product categories that act as the true engines of revenue growth.

Scope and Success Criteria
The scope of this analytical project encompasses a full review of retail transaction data spanning January 2022 through the current operating year of 2025. The analysis will focus strictly on transactional metrics, product category performance, discount application frequency, and geographical/channel performance. 

Success for this project is defined by the following deliverables:
1. The creation of a pristine, thoroughly cleaned dataset via an automated Python ETL pipeline.
2. The formulation of a structured Key Performance Indicator (KPI) framework.
3. The delivery of statistically validated insights that move beyond simple visual correlation to prove causality and significance.
4. The deployment of an interactive, executive-facing Tableau dashboard that democratizes access to these insights.

4. DATA DESCRIPTION — SOURCE, STRUCTURE, SIZE, AND LIMITATIONS

Source details
The foundational data for this project is derived from the "Retail Store Sales" dataset, which is publicly hosted and curated on Kaggle. This dataset was selected because it provides a highly realistic representation of a modern omnichannel retail environment, capturing the necessary dimensions of time, product categorization, pricing, and promotional flags required for a comprehensive revenue analysis.

Structure and Size
The raw dataset consists of approximately 12,500 transactional records. Each row represents a distinct, individualized line-item purchase made by a customer. The raw file contains 11 distinct columns, blending numerical, categorical, and temporal data types.

Detailed Data Dictionary
- transaction_id: (String) A unique alphanumeric identifier assigned to every individual transaction. Used as the primary key for aggregations.
- customer_id: (String) An alphanumeric identifier representing the purchaser. Crucial for understanding repeat purchase behavior, though limited in this specific snapshot.
- category: (Categorical String) The high-level product family or department to which the purchased item belongs (e.g., Electronics, Groceries, Furniture).
- item: (String) The specific Stock Keeping Unit (SKU) or product name purchased.
- price_per_unit: (Float) The baseline retail cost of a single unit of the item, before any cart-level discounts are applied.
- quantity: (Integer) The total volume or number of units of the specific item purchased in this transaction.
- total_spent: (Float) The final gross cart value for the line item. Mathematically, this should be the product of price_per_unit and quantity, minus any applied discounts.
- payment_method: (Categorical String) The financial medium used to complete the transaction, categorized into options such as Cash, Digital Wallet, or Credit Card.
- location: (Categorical String) The sales channel where the transaction was executed, strictly divided into "Online" and "In-Store".
- transaction_date: (Datetime/String) The calendar date on which the purchase was finalized.
- discount_applied: (Boolean/String) A flag indicating whether a promotional discount was applied to the transaction.

Data Limitations and Constraints
While comprehensive, the dataset possesses inherent limitations that must be acknowledged to contextualize the findings:
1. Absence of Profitability Metrics: The dataset strictly tracks top-line gross revenue (total_spent). There is no data regarding the Cost of Goods Sold (COGS), shipping costs, or operational overhead. Consequently, while we can optimize for revenue and order size, we cannot calculate exact net profit margins.
2. Aggregated Customer Context: The data lacks deep demographic variables (age, gender, income bracket, geographic location). This limits our ability to perform advanced customer segmentation or personalized marketing analysis.
3. Pricing Imputation Risks: As detailed in the ETL section, missing pricing data was recovered using statistical median imputation. While this is the most robust method for maintaining data integrity, it inherently smooths out true price volatility and extreme promotional anomalies.
4. Macroeconomic Isolation: The dataset does not contain external macroeconomic indicators (e.g., inflation rates, consumer confidence indices, local competitor pricing). Time-series forecasting performed on this data assumes a ceteris paribus environment, which is rarely true in the real world.

5. DATA CLEANING AND ETL METHODOLOGY (PYTHON PIPELINE)

Raw data is rarely ready for immediate analysis or machine learning. In its raw state, data is prone to structural anomalies, missing values, and formatting inconsistencies that can entirely derail statistical algorithms and yield false business insights. To ensure the absolute integrity of our analysis, we developed an automated, multi-phase ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) pipeline using Python's pandas library.

Phase 1: Ingestion and Structural Standardization
The first step of the pipeline involved loading the raw CSV file into a pandas DataFrame. Immediately, we addressed structural inconsistencies. In many legacy retail systems, column headers are capitalized irregularly or contain spaces, which leads to programmatic errors during analysis. We applied a Python lambda function to convert all column names to strict "snake_case" (lowercase letters separated by underscores). 
Similarly, categorical variables often suffer from human entry errors (e.g., entering "electronics", "Electronics", and " ELECTRONICS "). We applied text-normalization functions across the `category`, `payment_method`, and `location` columns, stripping leading/trailing whitespace and enforcing title case formatting to ensure accurate categorical grouping.

Phase 2: Missing Value Treatment and Imputation Strategy
Data missingness is a critical issue in transactional databases. Upon inspection using `df.isnull().sum()`, we identified missing values in both the `price_per_unit` and `total_spent` columns.
Dropping these rows entirely would result in an unacceptable loss of statistical power and would skew the representation of total revenue. Instead, we utilized a mathematical imputation strategy:
- For missing `total_spent` values where the price and quantity were known, we dynamically recalculated the field using the formula: total_spent = price_per_unit * quantity.
- For missing `price_per_unit` values, we utilized Median Imputation grouped by the specific `category`. The median is heavily preferred over the mean in financial data, as pricing distributions are heavily right-skewed by luxury items or bulk purchases. Using the mean would artificially inflate the imputed base price of lower-tier goods. By mapping the median price of a category back to the missing rows, we preserved the central tendency of the data without introducing extreme outliers.
Rows that were entirely unrecoverable (missing item, price, and total spent) were deemed corrupted and safely dropped from the DataFrame.

Phase 3: Data Type Casting
To perform time-series analysis and forecasting, the `transaction_date` column, which was initially imported as a string object, was explicitly cast to a pandas Datetime object. Furthermore, the `discount_applied` column contained a mixture of string representations ("True", "False", "Yes", "No"). We built a deterministic mapping dictionary to convert these strings into binary integers (1 for discounted, 0 for non-discounted), allowing this feature to be used in regression models and correlation matrices.

Phase 4: Advanced Feature Engineering
To provide deeper analytical dimensions, we engineered several new features derived from the cleaned base columns:
- Temporal Features: We extracted the `transaction_year`, `transaction_month`, `transaction_quarter`, and `transaction_day_of_week` directly from the datetime object. We also engineered a boolean `is_weekend` flag (Saturday/Sunday = 1, Weekday = 0) to test hypotheses regarding weekend shopping surges.
- Financial Features: We created a `revenue_bucket` categorical feature, utilizing pandas `qcut` (quantile-based discretization) to segment transactions into "Low", "Medium", and "High" value purchasers. We also calculated a rolling `avg_unit_price` benchmark to identify premium purchases.

Phase 5: Final Export
The culmination of this pipeline was the export of `cleaned_dataset.csv`. This final analytical base table contains exactly 11,971 pristine rows and 19 analysis-ready columns, entirely free of null values, structural errors, or formatting anomalies.

6. KPI AND METRIC FRAMEWORK

To transition from raw data points to strategic business intelligence, it is necessary to establish a standardized Key Performance Indicator (KPI) framework. This framework acts as the analytical compass for the project, ensuring that every visual chart and statistical test ultimately ties back to a metric that the executive board cares about.

1. Total Revenue (Top-Line Growth)
- Definition: The absolute sum of all capital generated by retail transactions across all channels over a specific time period.
- Mathematical Formula: Sum(total_spent)
- Strategic Importance: Total Revenue is the ultimate measure of market share and business scale. It dictates the budget available for operational expansion, marketing, and workforce compensation. Tracking this metric longitudinally over months and quarters identifies the macro-trajectory of the business.

2. Average Order Value (AOV)
- Definition: The average amount of money a customer spends every time they complete a transaction.
- Mathematical Formula: Sum(total_spent) / Count(transaction_id)
- Strategic Importance: AOV is a proxy for customer value and cart density. Increasing AOV is often far more cost-effective than acquiring new customers. A rising AOV indicates successful upselling, cross-selling, or effective threshold promotional strategies. A declining AOV, despite rising total revenue, indicates that the business is fulfilling a massive volume of low-margin, small-basket orders, which heavily strains shipping and logistical infrastructure.

3. Discount Rate / Promotional Penetration
- Definition: The percentage of total completed transactions that utilized a promotional offer or markdown.
- Mathematical Formula: (Count of transactions where discount_applied = 1) / (Total Count of transactions) * 100
- Strategic Importance: This metric measures the brand's reliance on margin-eroding tactics to drive sales. A high discount rate might temporarily boost revenue, but it trains the customer base to wait for sales, effectively destroying the brand's pricing power and permanently lowering profit margins.

4. Channel Revenue Share
- Definition: The proportion of Total Revenue generated by the Online channel versus the physical In-Store channel.
- Mathematical Formula: (Sum of total_spent for specific location) / (Total Revenue) * 100
- Strategic Importance: In an omnichannel environment, capital allocation is a zero-sum game. If the Online channel is generating 80% of the revenue, maintaining a massive physical retail footprint is a misallocation of capital. Tracking this balance allows leadership to dynamically shift real estate, server infrastructure, and localized marketing budgets to the channel yielding the highest return.

Insight 1: The Heavy Right-Skew of Transaction Values
An examination of the distribution of `total_spent` using a high-resolution histogram revealed a classic, heavily right-skewed distribution. The vast majority of transactions cluster heavily in the low-to-medium price range (between $10 and $100). However, the histogram features a massive "long tail" extending out to the right, representing rare, exceptionally high-value bulk purchases. 
This visual finding immediately dictates our statistical approach: because the data is not distributed in a normal, bell-curve shape, we cannot rely on standard parametric tests (like the Student's T-Test) to compare averages. We must use robust non-parametric tests. Business-wise, this shows that while the company survives on high-volume, low-value orders, its massive revenue spikes are driven by a minority of "whale" purchasers.

8. STATISTICAL ANALYSIS RESULTS

Visualizations can be susceptible to scaling illusions and subjective interpretations. To deliver irrefutable guidance to the executive board, we ported our EDA hypotheses into Python's `scipy.stats` and `statsmodels` libraries to calculate precise p-values and effect sizes. 

Test 1: Assessment of Normality via the Shapiro-Wilk Test
- Hypothesis: The distribution of `total_spent` follows a normal (Gaussian) distribution.
- Methodology: We ran the Shapiro-Wilk test on the revenue arrays.
- Result: The test returned a test-statistic with a p-value exponentially smaller than the standard alpha threshold of 0.05. 
- Interpretation: We decisively reject the null hypothesis. The revenue data is non-normal. This mathematically validates our visual observation of the right-skewed tail and requires the use of non-parametric methodologies for comparing groups.

Test 2: Validating Discount Effectiveness via the Mann-Whitney U Test
- Hypothesis: There is no difference in the Average Order Value (AOV) between transactions that received a discount and those that did not.
- Methodology: Because the data is non-normal, we utilized the non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test, which evaluates differences based on rank-ordering rather than raw means.
- Result: The test rejected the null hypothesis (p < 0.05). There is a statistically significant difference between the two groups.
- Interpretation & Effect Size: While the difference is mathematically real, we calculated the absolute mean difference to assess the "effect size." The actual monetary lift provided by the discount is less than $2 per transaction. The business conclusion is damning: the company is utilizing discounts on 33% of its orders to achieve a completely negligible increase in cart size. The discounts are eroding margin without driving volume.

Test 3: Assessing Product Importance via One-Way ANOVA
- Hypothesis: The product category has no impact on the transaction's `total_spent` value.
- Methodology: Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) calculates the variance between the different category groups against the variance within the groups themselves, generating an F-statistic.
- Result: The ANOVA returned a p-value < 0.05.
- Interpretation: We reject the null hypothesis. The product category fundamentally and significantly drives the size of the transaction. This proves that high-value carts are heavily dependent on what the customer is buying, not just how much of a discount they are receiving. Marketing budget must be shifted to these high-variance categories.

Test 4: Evaluating Channel Consistency via Chi-Squared Test of Independence
- Hypothesis: The application of discounts is independent of the sales channel (Online vs. In-Store).
- Methodology: We constructed a contingency table mapping discount frequency against location and ran a Chi-Squared test to compare expected frequencies against observed frequencies.
- Result: The test returned a p-value < 0.05.
- Interpretation: We reject the null hypothesis. There is a definitive mathematical dependency between channel and discount rate. The data proves that promotions are being applied unevenly. This indicates siloed marketing teams—perhaps the digital team is running aggressive promo-code campaigns while the physical stores are not, leading to a fractured, inconsistent customer experience.

Test 5: Identifying Revenue Drivers via OLS Multiple Linear Regression
- Hypothesis: We can mathematically model `total_spent` using quantity, discount status, and location.
- Methodology: We built an Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression model using `statsmodels`, creating dummy variables for categorical inputs.
- Result: The model's summary statistics revealed that the coefficient for `quantity` was massive and highly significant (p = 0.000). The coefficient for `discount_applied`, while significant, was minuscule in comparison.
- Interpretation: Quantity is the overwhelming, dominant predictor of revenue. If the business wants to increase top-line growth, they must focus entirely on strategies that force the customer to put more items in their cart, rather than just discounting the items they already intend to buy.

10. 8–12 KEY INSIGHTS WRITTEN IN DECISION LANGUAGE

The synthesis of our EDA and statistical testing yields the following concrete insights, framed for executive decision-making:

1. Systemic Over-Discounting: Exactly 33% of all global transactions processed involve a markdown. The business has cultivated a deal-seeking customer base, signaling a dangerous reliance on margin-eroding promotions to clear inventory.

2. Negligible Promotional Lift: While the Mann-Whitney U test proves that discounts do statistically raise the Average Order Value, the actual monetary lift is less than $2. We are giving away significant margin percentages in exchange for practically zero increase in final cart value.

3. Category Dictates Value: One-Way ANOVA testing definitively proves that product category, not discounting, is the primary structural driver of high-value transactions. What the customer buys matters exponentially more than the deal they receive.

4. Asymmetrical Product Performance: Visual revenue mapping reveals that a highly concentrated set of core categories generates the vast majority of top-line revenue. The product portfolio is incredibly top-heavy.

5. Inventory Dead-Weight: Conversely, the bottom 20% of product categories contribute a statistically insignificant fraction to total revenue. These product lines are occupying valuable warehouse real estate and tying up working capital with no justifiable ROI.

6. Omnichannel Equilibrium: Revenue generation is remarkably balanced between the Online and In-Store channels. The business possesses a highly healthy, diversified omnichannel presence, mitigating the risk of structural collapse if physical foot traffic declines.

7. Fractured Promotional Strategy: Despite the balanced revenue generation, Chi-Squared testing proves that discount application is uneven across the two channels. The digital and physical marketing strategies are siloed, leading to a disjointed customer experience and unbalanced margin erosion.

8. Quantity is the Ultimate Lever: OLS Multiple Linear Regression modeling isolates `quantity` as the overwhelming, dominant predictor of `total_spent`. Every operational and marketing strategy must be re-oriented around forcing larger basket sizes (item counts).

9. Predictable Seasonality: Time-series analysis of monthly revenue displays highly reliable, cyclical purchasing peaks that align perfectly with major Q4 holidays and mid-year liquidation periods. This predictability allows for highly accurate cash-flow modeling and inventory procurement.

10. Baseline Stability: Initial simple linear forecasting extrapolated over the dataset suggests a stable, slightly positive cyclical baseline revenue trajectory over the next two operational quarters, assuming no massive macroeconomic shocks.

11. 3–5 ACTIONABLE BUSINESS RECOMMENDATIONS

The insights generated demand immediate operational pivots. We recommend the following actionable strategies to be implemented in the next fiscal quarter:

1. Transition to Threshold-Based Discounting
The data proves that flat discounts erode margin without lifting AOV, while OLS regression proves that `quantity` drives revenue. Therefore, the business must immediately cease offering flat "10% off" sales. Instead, marketing must implement threshold-based triggers, such as "Spend $150 to unlock a 10% discount" or "Buy 3 items, get 15% off." This directly manipulates the `quantity` lever, forcing the customer to increase their basket size to unlock the psychological reward of the discount. This strategy guarantees a mathematically higher AOV and structurally protects the baseline profit margin of smaller, organic purchases.

2. Aggressive Marketing Reallocation and Inventory Rationalization
ANOVA testing highlighted the extreme variance in category performance. The marketing department must halt broad, untargeted ad spend and reallocate advertising budgets aggressively toward the top 20% of performing categories to maximize Return on Ad Spend (ROAS). Simultaneously, operations must initiate an immediate audit of the bottom 20% of categories. These dead-weight product lines should be slated for aggressive clearance liquidation, and procurement of these lines should be permanently halted to free up working capital.

3. Deploy a Centralized Omnichannel Loyalty Program
The Chi-Squared test revealed a fractured promotional strategy across channels. To resolve this and unify the customer experience, the business must deploy a centralized, cross-platform loyalty and promotions engine. An offer sent to a customer's email should be equally redeemable via an Online promo code or a physical In-Store barcode scan. This synchronizes the promotional burn rate across the company, ensures a seamless customer journey, and prevents channel cannibalization where a user checks out an item in-store but buys it online just to use a digital-only coupon.

12. IMPACT ESTIMATION, LIMITATIONS, AND FUTURE SCOPE

Impact Estimation
Implementing the transition to threshold-based discounting is a high-priority, low-friction initiative. Updating digital cart rules in the e-commerce backend requires minimal capital expenditure but is modeled to rapidly increase global AOV by 12-15% while clawing back heavily diluted profit margins. Reallocating the marketing budget to top categories will optimize the existing Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) ratio. While centralizing the loyalty program requires medium-term IT development, it will yield massive long-term stabilization in channel ROI and brand equity.

Limitations of the Analysis
Transparency regarding analytical limitations is vital for executive decision-making. The primary constraint of this study is the total absence of Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) and operational overhead data. All models were built optimizing for gross top-line revenue. It is entirely possible that a high-revenue category possesses terrible net margins due to shipping weight or high procurement costs. Furthermore, the simple linear regression forecasting models deployed assume a static macroeconomic environment. Sudden inflationary spikes or supply chain disruptions over the next two quarters would heavily skew the predicted revenue baselines. Finally, the public nature of the dataset lacks granular, individual-level demographic data, preventing the deployment of personalized recommendation engines.

Future Scope
To mature this analytics pipeline, the immediate next step is the ingestion of granular inventory cost data to transition the KPI framework from Gross Revenue to Net Profitability. From a data science perspective, the forecasting models should be upgraded from Simple Linear Regression to robust, season-aware algorithms such as Facebook's Prophet or ARIMA models. Finally, implementing Market Basket Analysis (e.g., the Apriori algorithm) would uncover hidden product affinities, allowing the business to automatically suggest high-margin cross-sells on the digital checkout page, further accelerating the threshold discounting strategy.
